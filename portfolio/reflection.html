Lessons Learned

Challenges and Triumphs

Going into this course, I already had some programming experience with Java and Python, but I had never written HTML or CSS before. The first few labs were still challenging because web development requires a different way of thinking. I was not writing logic or functions anymore. I was writing code that controlled the structure and layout of a webpage. Remembering tags, closing elements correctly, and understanding how nesting worked took time to get used to. Things started to make more sense once I understood the box model and how padding, margin, and borders affect layout. That was probably one of the biggest turning points for me because I finally understood why elements moved and spaced themselves the way they did on a page.

Testing on Multiple Browsers and Devices

I tested my pages on Chrome, Safari, and my iPhone to make sure everything worked correctly across different devices. One thing I noticed was that the hamburger menu I built for Lab 08 behaved a little differently on mobile Safari. The sticky header sometimes caused scrolling issues that never appeared on desktop Chrome. That experience showed me why mobile testing matters instead of only resizing a browser window on a computer. Overall, the layouts worked well across both browsers, but the experience taught me that responsive design is not only about screen size. It is also about touch interaction, loading speed, and how different browsers handle the same code.

What I Learned in This Course

CSIT 121 gave me a foundation that ended up being much more useful than I originally expected. Before taking the course, I thought web development was mostly separate from my interest in business analysis and data. As the semester went on, I started to realize how connected those areas actually are. One of the biggest examples was the CSV to JSON web standard I researched in Lab 10. That assignment helped me understand that websites are not just about appearance and design. They are also used to organize, transfer, and present structured data, which connects closely to data analysis and business systems. I also gained a much better understanding of accessibility. Running an accessibility audit on UMGC's website and finding real issues like incorrect heading order and missing landmarks made the topic feel practical instead of just theoretical. It changed the way I look at websites now because I pay more attention to usability and structure. Looking back at all ten labs together, I can clearly see my progress throughout the semester. I started with a single HTML page with almost no styling, and by the end of the course I was building multi-page websites with JavaScript, XML parsing, responsive layouts, and more organized design choices. The amount I learned in one semester surprised me, and I feel much more confident using these skills in future courses and projects.
